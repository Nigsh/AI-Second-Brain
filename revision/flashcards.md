### Introduction to Electrocardiography

**Q:** What is Electrocardiography?
**A:** The process of using an electrocardiograph (device) to produce an electrocardiogram (recording, ECG/EKG) showing a line graph of the heart's electrical activity.

**Q:** What is an Electrocardiograph?
**A:** The device used to produce an electrocardiogram.

**Q:** What is an Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG)?
**A:** A recording that shows a line graph of the heart's electrical activity over repeated cardiac cycles. It's a graph of voltage versus time of the heart's electrical activity.

**Q:** How do electrodes on the skin detect cardiac electrical activity?
**A:** They detect small electrical changes resulting from cardiac muscle depolarization followed by repolarization during each heartbeat.

**Q:** List some cardiac abnormalities that can cause changes in the normal ECG pattern.
**A:** Cardiac rhythm disturbances (e.g., atrial fibrillation), inadequate coronary artery blood flow (e.g., myocardial ischemia), and electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalemia).

**Q:** What is typically meant by "ECG" in a traditional medical context?
**A:** A 12-lead ECG taken while a patient is lying down.

### 12-Lead ECG Components

**Q:** How many electrodes and leads are used in a conventional 12-lead ECG?
**A:** Ten electrodes are placed on the patient's limbs and chest, measuring electrical potential from twelve different angles ("leads").

**Q:** What are the three main components of an ECG tracing?
**A:** The P wave, the QRS complex, and the T wave.

**Q:** What does the P wave represent?
**A:** Depolarization of the atria.

**Q:** What does the QRS complex represent?
**A:** Depolarization of the ventricles.

**Q:** What does the T wave represent?
**A:** Repolarization of the ventricles.

**Q:** Describe the orderly progression of depolarization in a healthy heart.
**A:** Starts in pacemaker cells of the sinoatrial (SA) node, spreads throughout the atrium, passes through the atrioventricular (AV) node into the bundle of His, and then into the Purkinje fibers, spreading throughout the ventricles.

### Medical Uses of ECG

**Q:** List some medical indications for performing an ECG.
**A:** Chest pain/suspected myocardial infarction, symptoms like shortness of breath/fainting/palpitations, medication monitoring, electrolyte abnormalities, perioperative monitoring, cardiac stress testing, and gating for cardiac imaging (CTA/MRA).

**Q:** What is the difference between intermittent and continuous ECG monitoring?
**A:** Intermittent is short tracings (e.g., conventional 10-second ECG); continuous is used for critically ill patients or infrequent arrhythmias (e.g., Holter monitors, biotelemetry).

### ECG Screening & Technology

**Q:** Is ECG screening generally recommended for asymptomatic adults at low risk of cardiovascular disease?
**A:** No, evidence does not support it due to potential for false positives, misdiagnosis, and overtreatment.

**Q:** Who might be required to have an ECG as part of routine health evaluations or screening?
**A:** Persons in critical occupations (e.g., aircraft pilots) or adolescents for hypertrophic cardiomyopathy screening in sports physicals.

**Q:** Who developed the first practical ECG using the string galvanometer?
**A:** Willem Einthoven (1901), who received the Nobel Prize in 1924.

**Q:** What are some modern advancements in electrocardiograph machines?
**A:** Portability, inclusion in fitness trackers and smartwatches (often single-lead), analog-to-digital conversion, automated interpretation algorithms, and safety features like defibrillation protection.

**Q:** What is the main limitation of automated ECG interpretation algorithms?
**A:** They are considered "preliminary" and can have misinterpretations, leading to potential clinical mismanagement if not verified by an expert.

### ECG Electrodes and Leads

**Q:** What is the difference between an electrode and a lead in ECG?
**A:** **Electrodes** are the actual conductive pads attached to the body surface. A **lead** measures the electrical potential difference between two electrodes or between an electrode and a virtual electrode.

**Q:** What is Wilson's Central Terminal (WCT)?
**A:** A virtual electrode whose potential is defined as the average potential measured by the three standard limb leads (RA, LA, LL). It serves as the negative pole for precordial leads.

**Q:** What are the three types of leads in a 12-lead ECG?
**A:** Limb leads, augmented limb leads, and precordial (chest) leads.

**Q:** Name the four limb electrodes and their standard AHA colors/placement.
**A:**
*   **RA (Right Arm):** White, right arm below shoulder.
*   **LA (Left Arm):** Black, symmetrical to RA.
*   **RL (Right Leg):** Green, right leg below hips (neutral/ground).
*   **LL (Left Leg):** Red, symmetrical to RL.

**Q:** Name the six precordial electrodes (V1-V6) and their general placement.
**A:**
*   **V1:** Fourth intercostal space, right sternal border.
*   **V2:** Fourth intercostal space, left sternal border.
*   **V3:** Halfway between V2 and V4.
*   **V4:** Fifth intercostal space, midclavicular line.
*   **V5:** Left anterior axillary line, same horizontal plane as V4.
*   **V6:** Left midaxillary line, same horizontal plane as V4/V5.

**Q:** What are the two types of common ECG electrodes made of?
**A:** An electrically conductive electrolyte gel and a silver/silver chloride (Ag/AgCl) conductor.

### ECG Lead Views and Calculations

**Q:** Name the three standard limb leads and their voltage calculations.
**A:**
*   **Lead I:** LA - RA
*   **Lead II:** LL - RA
*   **Lead III:** LL - LA

**Q:** Name the three augmented limb leads.
**A:** aVR, aVL, aVF.

**Q:** How are augmented limb leads derived, and what modification is used?
**A:** They are derived from the same three limb electrodes but use Goldberger's central terminal as their negative pole, which is a combination of two limb electrodes for each augmented lead (producing 50% larger amplitudes).

**Q:** What plane do the limb and augmented limb leads view the heart from?
**A:** The coronal (vertical) plane.

**Q:** What plane do the precordial leads view the heart from?
**A:** The transverse (horizontal) plane.

**Q:** What is the hexaxial reference system used for?
**A:** To calculate the heart's electrical axis in the frontal plane, using leads I, II, III, aVR, aVL, and aVF.

### Specialized ECG Leads and Reports

**Q:** When might right-sided precordial leads (e.g., V5R) or posterior leads (V7-V9) be used?
**A:** Right-sided for right ventricle pathology/dextrocardia; posterior for posterior myocardial infarction.

**Q:** What is an esophageal lead used for?
**A:** More accurate differentiation of certain cardiac arrhythmias (e.g., atrial flutter) and evaluation of Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome.

**Q:** What is an intracardiac electrogram (ICEG)?
**A:** An ECG with added intracardiac leads (inside the heart) via cardiac catheterization.

**Q:** How are tracings typically arranged on a standard 12-lead ECG report?
**A:** A grid of four columns and three rows, often with a rhythm strip as a fourth or fifth row. The first column typically shows limb leads (I, II, III), the second augmented limb leads (aVR, aVL, aVF), and the last two precordial leads (V1-V6).

### Contiguous Leads and Electrophysiology

**Q:** What does it mean for ECG leads to be "contiguous"?
**A:** They look at neighboring anatomical areas of the heart.

**Q:** Which leads are considered inferior leads and what area do they view?
**A:** Leads II, III, and aVF; they view the inferior (diaphragmatic) surface of the heart.

**Q:** Which leads are considered lateral leads and what area do they view?
**A:** Leads I, aVL, V5, and V6; they view the lateral wall of the left ventricle.

**Q:** Which leads are considered septal leads and what area do they view?
**A:** Leads V1 and V2; they view the septal surface (interventricular septum) of the heart.

**Q:** Which leads are considered anterior leads and what area do they view?
**A:** Leads V3 and V4; they view the anterior wall of the right and left ventricles.

**Q:** What is cardiac electrophysiology (EP)?
**A:** The study of the conduction system of the heart, often performed via right-sided cardiac catheterization to record electrical activity from within the heart.

### ECG Interpretation Theory

**Q:** What is the fundamental principle of ECG interpretation?
**A:** Understanding the electrical conduction system of the heart.

**Q:** Does an ECG directly show the mechanical pumping activity of the heart?
**A:** No, an ECG shows electrical activity. For example, pulseless electrical activity shows an ECG without mechanical output, and ventricular fibrillation shows dysfunctional electrical activity without life-sustaining output.

**Q:** What are the four theoretical points linking electrical activity to ECG deflection?
**A:**
1.  Depolarization toward a positive electrode = positive deflection.
2.  Depolarization away from a positive electrode = negative deflection.
3.  Repolarization toward a positive electrode = negative deflection.
4.  Repolarization away from a positive electrode = positive deflection.

**Q:** What is an isoelectric deflection?
**A:** Minimal deflection on an ECG lead when the depolarization/repolarization vector is perpendicular to that lead's vector.

**Q:** What does the U wave on an ECG represent (if seen)?
**A:** Hypothesized to be papillary muscle repolarization or interventricular septum repolarization.

**Q:** Why is atrial repolarization typically not seen on a standard ECG?
**A:** It is hidden within the much more prominent QRS complex due to the larger muscle mass of the ventricles.

### Rate and Rhythm Interpretation

**Q:** What is considered a normal resting heart rate (normocardic) for adults?
**A:** Between 60 and 100 beats per minute (bpm).

**Q:** What are bradycardia and tachycardia defined as in adults?
**A:** Bradycardia: <60 bpm. Tachycardia: >100 bpm.

**Q:** What is the physiologic rhythm of a normal resting heart?
**A:** Normal sinus rhythm (NSR).

**Q:** What is a key criterion for determining if an ECG shows a sinus rhythm?
**A:** P waves and QRS complexes appear in a 1-to-1 relationship, implying the P wave causes the QRS complex.

**Q:** What ECG finding is a hallmark of atrial fibrillation?
**A:** Absent P waves with "irregularly irregular" QRS complexes.

**Q:** What ECG finding is a hallmark of atrial flutter?
**A:** A "saw tooth" pattern with QRS complexes.

### QRS Axis Interpretation

**Q:** What does the QRS axis represent?
**A:** The general direction of the ventricular depolarization wavefront (mean electrical vector) in the frontal plane.

**Q:** What is the typical range for a normal QRS axis in the frontal plane?
**A:** -30° to 105°.

**Q:** What indicates right axis deviation?
**A:** QRS axis beyond +105°.

**Q:** What indicates left axis deviation?
**A:** QRS axis beyond -30°.

**Q:** What is a quick shortcut for determining if the QRS axis is normal?
**A:** If the QRS complex is mostly positive in both Lead I and Lead II.

### ECG Waveforms and Intervals

**Q:** What is the normal duration of a P wave?
**A:** <80 ms.

**Q:** What does a P wave with unusually long duration or a two-humped (bifid) shape indicate?
**A:** Atrial enlargement (long duration, or large left atrium for bifid).

**Q:** What is the PR interval and its normal duration?
**A:** Measured from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex; reflects SA-AV node conduction time. Normal duration: 120 to 200 ms.

**Q:** What does a PR interval shorter than 120 ms suggest?
**A:** Electrical impulse bypassing the AV node (e.g., Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome).

**Q:** What does a PR interval consistently longer than 200 ms diagnose?
**A:** First-degree atrioventricular block.

**Q:** What is the normal duration of the QRS complex?
**A:** 80 to 100 ms.

**Q:** What does a wide QRS complex (longer than 120 ms) suggest?
**A:** Disruption of the heart's conduction system (e.g., LBBB, RBBB, ventricular rhythms) or metabolic issues (e.g., hyperkalemia).

**Q:** What is the J-point?
**A:** The point at which the QRS complex finishes and the ST segment begins.

**Q:** What does the ST segment represent?
**A:** The period when the ventricles are depolarized (usually isoelectric).

**Q:** What can ST depression or elevation indicate?
**A:** Myocardial infarction or ischemia, pericarditis, Brugada syndrome, or normal variant (J-point elevation for ST elevation), or LVH/digoxin for ST depression.

**Q:** What does the T wave represent?
**A:** Repolarization of the ventricles.

**Q:** What can inverted T waves be a sign of?
**A:** Myocardial ischemia, left ventricular hypertrophy, high intracranial pressure, or metabolic abnormalities.

**Q:** What can peaked T waves be a sign of?
**A:** Hyperkalemia or very early myocardial infarction.

**Q:** What is the Corrected QT interval (QTc) and why is it "corrected"?
**A:** Measured from the beginning of the QRS to the end of the T wave. It's corrected for heart rate because acceptable ranges vary with heart rate.

**Q:** What is a prolonged QTc interval a risk factor for?
**A:** Ventricular tachyarrhythmias and sudden death.

**Q:** What can a prominent U wave be a sign of?
**A:** Hypokalemia, hypercalcemia, or hyperthyroidism.

### Conduction and Waveform Formation

**Q:** What happens during the PR segment of the ECG?
**A:** Depolarization travels through the AV node, which is too small for its electrical activity to be detected on most ECGs, creating a flat segment.

**Q:** How does depolarization of the interventricular septum contribute to the QRS complex?
**A:** It spreads from left to right (due to bundle branch timing), potentially causing a negative deflection (Q wave) in some leads.

**Q:** What generates the R wave on the ECG?
**A:** Depolarization traveling towards the apex of the heart, resulting in a positive deflection on limb leads.

**Q:** What generates the S wave on the ECG?
**A:** Depolarization traveling throughout both ventricles from the apex, creating a negative deflection in limb leads.

**Q:** Why does repolarization of the ventricles (T wave) result in a positive deflection despite being a spread of negative current?
**A:** Repolarization starts from the apex and moves upwards, and it's the spread of *negative* current (as membrane potentials decrease), so the vector points opposite to repolarization, causing a positive deflection.

### Ischemia and Infarction

**Q:** How might ischemia or non-ST elevation myocardial infarctions (NSTEMIs) manifest on an ECG?
**A:** ST depression or inversion of T waves.

**Q:** What is the earliest ECG sign of an ST elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI)?
**A:** Hyperacute T waves (peaked T waves).

**Q:** What ECG changes follow hyperacute T waves in a STEMI over minutes to hours?
**A:** Elevations of the ST segment (at least 1 mm), followed by a pathologic Q wave and T wave inversion over hours.

**Q:** Which coronary artery occlusion is suggested by ST elevations in anterior leads (V1, V2)?
**A:** Left Anterior Descending (LAD) artery.

**Q:** Which coronary artery occlusion is suggested by ST elevations in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6)?
**A:** Left Circumflex (LCx) artery.

**Q:** Which coronary artery occlusion is suggested by ST elevations in inferior leads (II, III, aVF)?
**A:** Right Coronary Artery (RCA).

### ECG Artifacts and Diagnosis

**Q:** What are ECG artifacts?
**A:** Distorted signals caused by secondary internal or external sources, such as muscle movement (shivering/tremors) or electrical interference.

**Q:** Why is proper lead placement crucial for ECG interpretation?
**A:** Improper lead placement (e.g., reversing limb leads) can lead to erroneous analysis, misdiagnosis, and inappropriate treatment.

**Q:** What is the hallmark ECG finding for atrial fibrillation?
**A:** Irregularly irregular QRS complexes without P waves.

**Q:** What ECG findings are associated with hyperkalemia?
**A:** Peaked T waves, widened QRS complexes, and loss of P waves.
